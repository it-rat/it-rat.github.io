on call · the design, not built yet

The alert finds you anywhere. Acting on it only works from inside.

Two different things get bundled together and should not be: being told something happened, and being able to do something about it. They need opposite things from the network, and pretending otherwise is how a pager ends up needing the very connection that is down.

Being told needs nothing open

When a run crosses its cap, your box sends the alert out by mail. Outbound only: no inbound route to your network, no relay in the middle, no app to install and no developer account to hold. Your phone gets it the way it gets any mail, with the tunnel down and nothing running. An alert that only arrives once you are already connected is not an alert, it is a dashboard you have to remember to open.

Acting needs you inside

The mail carries a deep link, and the link goes to your console, which lives on your box and answers only over your own WireGuard tunnel. Phone on the tunnel, console opens, you sign in, and the destructive step is signed again with a passkey on that device. So a stolen alert is worth nothing: what it points at cannot be reached from the internet, and the action needs a key that never left your hand.

Nothing to spoof, nothing to trust

You may wonder how the browser knows it reached your box and not something wearing its name. It cannot reach anything else: the address only exists inside your WireGuard network, and WireGuard authenticates the peer by key before a single byte of HTTP moves. There is no public name to impersonate, no certificate authority in the decision and nothing to buy. The tunnel is not carrying the encryption on top of the trust; it is the trust.

Why the mail has no Kill button

A button in an email is a URL, and URLs in mail get fetched by scanners, previews and clients before any human sees them. Anything that acts when opened will eventually act on its own. So the mail never carries a capability: it carries a link to a page that asks who you are and then asks your device to sign.

The phone, without an app

Your box issues a WireGuard config as a QR code, and it is scanned by the official WireGuard app rather than one of ours. That is the whole mobile requirement: no App Store review, no notarization, nothing of ours on the device. Reaching your console away from the tunnel is a relay problem we have deliberately not solved yet, and until we do, off-tunnel means you read the alert now and act when you are back on.

felyx · the copilot

An AI that reads everything and can touch nothing.

Felyx is the fleet's analyst. It answers money questions with numbers pulled by tools, chains an incident across money, identity, policy and memory into a root cause with cited ids, drafts the kill you were about to write, and annotates the page that wakes you. It is also an AI we can honestly ask you to trust, because the trust is structural, not a system prompt.

Local first, by default

Out of the box Felyx talks to a model on your own hardware: Ollama, LM Studio, vLLM - any OpenAI-compatible endpoint on localhost or your private network. A residency gate refuses any other destination, so a sensitive install cannot leak a prompt or a number by misconfiguration.

Cloud only if you choose it

An operator can explicitly opt into a bring-your-own-key cloud model - Anthropic or OpenRouter - per install, in config, off by default. The app's residency banner always states which mode you are in; there is no silent fallback.

Numbers from tools, never vibes

Every figure in an answer comes from a typed read tool over the same connectors the tabs use. The model does not do arithmetic in prose, and when a plane is not configured Felyx says what it cannot see instead of inventing it.

No signing key. Not restrained - absent.

The copilot crate has no dependency on the signing crate, and a build-time test asserts it stays that way. A proposal becomes action only through the same Touch ID ceremony a human uses; the audit trail reads “human approved copilot proposal”, never “copilot did it”.

It cannot silence the pager

In that design, the hard floor is deterministic code: an over-cap or runaway event fires immediately, before any model is called. Felyx may only add a short annotation inside a strict time budget. A slow, wrong or absent model changes nothing about whether you get paged.

Metered like any other agent

Felyx's own LLM calls route through TokenFuse under their own run id, so the assistant that watches your budgets has a budget: visible, cappable, killable. The thesis, dogfooded.

the technology

Two shells. One Rust brain. Hardware where it counts.

One core, one shell that ships

The browser console is the product. A Tauri desktop build is kept green beside it, unshipped: two shells over one shared Rust core is what forces the logic - stores, connectors, ceremonies, the graph - to live in the core rather than drifting into a screen. Written once, tested once, so a command cannot mean one thing in one place and something else in another.

Hardware signatures

The direction: kills and budget changes ES256-signed by a passkey on your own device (Touch ID, Windows Hello or a security key), with a typed break-glass reason journaled next to the signature, so a stolen laptop session or a compromised server cannot forge an order. Not shipped yet: today every kill is software-signed, and the hardware ceremony is proven in the unshipped desktop build.

WireGuard, but yours

Your box runs the WireGuard side and issues the peer config as a QR; the tunnel is raised by your own client, not by anything of ours: scan, connect, done. Your control plane answers on a private address and nowhere else. SSH stays for ops; the console does not need it.

A relay that cannot act

The pager's relay, in the design we explored, holds a read-only viewer key, binds to exactly one device, pins its TLS identity into the pairing QR, and forwards device-signed mutations byte-for-byte. Compromise the relay and you can watch: you still cannot kill, budget or grant.

Post-quantum licensing

Licenses are offline entitlement files signed with ML-DSA, the NIST post-quantum standard. No license server, no phone-home: it works in an air-gapped room, which is exactly where some of this software will live.

Self-hosted, your cloud or on-prem

We never run your Cloud plane or hold your data. The plane and relay live on infrastructure you own - AWS, GCP, Hetzner, any cloud or on-prem - reached only over WireGuard. Nothing to subpoena or breach on our side.
